Tunisian Law on Violence Against Women

Tunisia has been a pioneer in women's rights in the Arab world, with progressive legislation aimed at protecting women from violence and discrimination.

Law No. 2017-58 on Eliminating Violence Against Women

This landmark legislation, adopted in 2017, represents a comprehensive approach to combating violence against women in Tunisia.

Key Provisions of the Law

  • Comprehensive Definition of Violence: The law defines violence against women as "any physical, moral, sexual or economic aggression" against women based on discrimination between the two sexes.
  • Criminalization of Marital Rape: For the first time in Tunisian history, the law explicitly criminalizes marital rape.
  • Protection Orders: The law introduces protection orders that can be issued by courts to protect women from their aggressors.
  • Economic Violence: The legislation recognizes economic violence, including deprivation of resources and preventing women from working.
  • Strengthened Penalties: The law increases penalties for sexual harassment in public spaces and workplaces.
  • Support Services: It mandates the establishment of specialized units within police stations and courts to handle cases of violence against women.

Forms of Violence

  • Domestic Violence: The most common form, often underreported due to social stigma and economic dependence.
  • Sexual Harassment: Prevalent in public spaces and workplaces, despite legal prohibitions.
  • Economic Violence: Includes controlling women's access to financial resources and employment.
  • Psychological Violence: Verbal abuse, threats, and controlling behaviors that undermine women's self-esteem.

Types of Violence Faced by Women with Disabilities

Physical Violence

  • Hitting, pushing, or physical restraint
  • Withholding necessary assistive devices
  • Forced medication or medical procedures

Psychological Violence

  • Verbal abuse and humiliation related to disability
  • Isolation from family and community
  • Threats of institutionalization

Sexual Violence

  • Higher rates of sexual assault
  • Forced sterilization
  • Reproductive coercion

Economic Violence

  • Withholding disability benefits
  • Preventing access to employment
  • Financial exploitation

Legal Protections

Law 58 (2017) specifically includes protections for women with disabilities, recognizing their particular vulnerability to violence. The law mandates accessibility in shelters and support services.

Economic Integration Programs

Several programs aim to economically empower women with disabilities in Tunisia:

  • Vocational training programs adapted for different disabilities
  • Micro-credit initiatives specifically for women with disabilities
  • Quota systems in public sector employment
  • Supported employment programs
  • Entrepreneurship grants and business development support

Support Resources for Victims of Violence

National Green Line for Reporting Violence

1899

Free, confidential, available 24/7 for reporting violence and accessing support services

Specialized Units

Tunisia has established specialized units to handle cases of violence against women:

Specialized Police Units

  • Located in main police stations across Tunisia
  • Specially trained female officers
  • Confidential reporting procedures
  • Immediate protection measures

Regional Coordination Units

  • Coordinate between different support services
  • Available in all 24 governorates
  • Multi-disciplinary teams (legal, psychological, social)
  • Case management and follow-up

Specialized Courts

  • Dedicated judges for violence cases
  • Expedited procedures
  • Protection orders available
  • Victim-friendly courtrooms

Additional Support Services

Key Observations

  • Tunisia stands out in the MENA region for its progressive legislation on women's rights.
  • Despite strong laws, implementation gaps mean violence rates remain high compared to countries with similar legal frameworks.
  • Cultural attitudes and economic factors significantly influence the effectiveness of legal protections.
  • Tunisia's approach combines legal reform with awareness campaigns, similar to strategies in Scandinavian countries.
